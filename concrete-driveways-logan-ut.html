Concrete Driveways Built for Cache Valley Winters

A driveway in Cache Valley takes a beating. Between heavy snow load, road salt, and the constant freeze-and-thaw that runs from fall through spring, a poorly poured slab can crack, spall, or heave within just a few seasons. The difference between a driveway that lasts 5 years and one that lasts 30 comes down to what happens before the concrete is ever poured — the base prep, drainage, slab thickness, reinforcement, the air-entrained mix, and properly placed control joints.

We install residential and commercial driveways throughout Logan, North Logan, Smithfield, Providence, Hyrum, Nibley, Hyde Park, Wellsville and the surrounding Cache Valley communities — and we build every one specifically for our local climate, not from a generic template.

What Does a Concrete Driveway Cost in Logan?

It's the first question almost everyone asks, and the honest answer is that it depends on your specific project. As a rough guide, most residential concrete driveways in the Cache Valley area run somewhere around $7 to $13 per square foot installed for a standard finish, which puts a typical two-car driveway in the low-to-mid four figures. A simple slab on a well-prepped lot costs less; decorative finishes cost more.

The biggest factors that move your price up or down are:

  • Square footage — the single largest driver of total cost.
  • Slab thickness — 4″ for cars vs. 5–6″ for trucks, RVs and trailers.
  • Tear-out — removing and hauling away an old driveway adds labor and disposal.
  • Site & base prep — grading, drainage, and how much gravel base your soil needs.
  • Reinforcement — rebar or wire mesh sized to the load.
  • Finish — broom is the most affordable; stamped, colored and exposed-aggregate cost more.

Because every lot and soil type is different, the only way to get a real number is an on-site look. We give you a clear, written estimate for free — no guessing over the phone and no pressure.

Our Driveway Installation Process

"Pouring concrete" is the easy part — the longevity is in the steps most people never see. Here's how we build a driveway that holds up to Utah winters:

  1. Free on-site estimate. We measure, check your soil and drainage, talk through finishes, and give you a written price.
  2. Excavation & grading. We remove old concrete or sod, then grade the site so water drains away from your home and slab.
  3. Compacted gravel base. Concrete should never sit on bare dirt. We lay and mechanically compact a gravel sub-base so the slab won't settle.
  4. Forming. We set forms to the right slope and dimensions for clean edges and proper runoff.
  5. Reinforcement. We place rebar or mesh sized to the load, with extra reinforcement where heavy vehicles park.
  6. Pour & finish. We pour an air-entrained mix (critical for freeze-thaw), then float and finish the surface in your chosen texture.
  7. Control joints. We cut joints at the correct spacing so the slab cracks where we decide, not randomly across your driveway.
  8. Curing & sealing. We cure it properly — which in our dry climate is what protects the surface — and seal it to resist salt and moisture.

Repair or Replace? Signs Your Driveway Is Failing

Not every driveway needs a full tear-out. Hairline cracks and minor settling can often be fixed. But it's usually time to replace when you see deep or widening cracks, large sunken sections, surface spalling (the top layer flaking off), or water that now pools toward your garage or foundation. If your slab has simply sunk but is otherwise solid, concrete leveling and repair may save you money over a full replacement — we'll give you an honest recommendation either way.

Making Your New Driveway Last

A quality pour is most of the battle, but a little maintenance goes a long way in our climate. Reseal your driveway every few years to keep moisture and salt out of the surface, go easy on harsh deicers (especially the first winter — they accelerate spalling), and keep standing water from collecting on or beside the slab. Done right, a concrete driveway here regularly lasts 25–30 years or more.

Driveway FAQ

How much does a concrete driveway cost in Logan, UT?

Most standard residential driveways run roughly $7–$13 per square foot installed, so a typical two-car driveway lands in the low-to-mid four figures. Square footage, thickness, tear-out, base prep, reinforcement and finish all affect the final price, and a broom finish costs less than stamped or colored concrete. We give a free written estimate after seeing the site.

How thick should my driveway be?

Four inches is standard for passenger vehicles. We recommend 5 to 6 inches anywhere trucks, RVs or trailers will sit. Base prep and reinforcement matter just as much as thickness for surviving freeze-thaw.

How long does a concrete driveway last in Utah?

A properly installed, sealed concrete driveway commonly lasts 25–30 years or more here — often outlasting asphalt by a wide margin. The keys are good base prep, an air-entrained mix, correct joints, and occasional resealing.

Do you remove my old driveway?

Yes. Full tear-out and haul-away of the old slab is part of a replacement, and we re-prep and compact the base before pouring the new driveway — we never pour over a failing slab or bare dirt.

How long does the job take?

Most residential driveways take about 2 to 4 days of on-site work. You can usually walk on the new slab within a day, then wait while it cures to full strength before driving on it.

When is the best time to pour in Utah?

Late spring through early fall is ideal, while temperatures stay above freezing during curing. We can pour in cooler weather with blankets and mix adjustments, but we won't pour into a hard freeze.

How soon can I drive on it?

Walk on it after about 24 hours, drive a car on it after roughly 7 days, and wait around 28 days before parking heavy vehicles so it reaches full strength.

Do I need a permit for a driveway in Logan?

It varies by city. Many projects — especially a new approach where the driveway meets the public street — require a permit, while a straight replacement on existing private property often doesn't. We'll tell you what your specific project needs.

Concrete or asphalt — which is better here?

Both work, but concrete typically lasts longer, handles our freeze-thaw and summer heat with less rutting, needs less frequent maintenance, and adds more resale value. Asphalt is usually cheaper up front. For most Cache Valley homeowners, concrete is the better long-term value.
